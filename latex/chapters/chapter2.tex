\chapter{X-ray Detectors}
\label{chap:chapter2}

Since its birth in the 1960s \cite{giacconi2003}, X-ray astronomy has represented an important \fix{opportunity} to study high-energy phenomena that involve celestial objects across all the universe. The possibility to observe photons produced both by galactic and extragalactic sources, such as neutron stars, bynary sistems, supernova remnants or Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN), is made possible by the small probability of interaction with the low-density interstellar medium (ISM) and intergalactic medium (IGM) for energies above 0.1-1 keV. The main problem of X-ray astronomy arises when the photon reaches Earth's atmosphere, which is much denser than ISM and IGM, thus photons are easily absorbed and can not reach the surface. Just for comparison, atmosphere's outer layers have a density of about $10^{17}$ atoms cm$^{-3}$, while for ISM the average on the galactic plane is $1$ atoms cm$^{-3}$ and for IGM is $10^{6}$ atoms cm$^{-3}$ \cite{longair2011}. The presence of the atmosphere makes it necessary to build space telescopes on board of satellites and send them into orbit around the Earth or the Sun. This is the reason why X-ray astronomy was born only after the progress of rocket science, when it became possible to launch the first detectors on sub-orbital flights, with which the first extrasolar sources were discovered, and then on satellites, which allowed to improve observations thanks to the increased observation time and to fully dedicates missions. 

This opportunity allowed to access a new perspective on the universe, which led to the discovery of new types of sources and to a better understanding of the physical processes that take place in astrophysical sources during high-energy events. An example is Scorpius X-1, the first detected extrasolar X-ray source and the most luminous persistent source of the X-ray sky, which has an X-ray luminosity about $10^3$ times larger than its optical counterpart, indicating that the emission processes are very different from those of the Sun, where the X-ray luminosity is about $10^{-6}$ smaller than the visible light luminosity. Indeed, Scorpius X-1 is a Low Mass X-ray Binary system (LMXB) where a compact object, a neutron star in this case, accretes material from its companion and the emission is mainly due to the conversion of gravitational energy into thermal energy. Many other sources similar to Scorpius X-1 and many different ones were discovered in later years, such as active galactic nuclei, which are extragalactic sources where a supermassive black hole with a mass larger than $10^6$ M$_{\odot}$ accretes matter from the surrounding environment. All of these high-energy sources are united by the presence of extreme conditions, with gas heated to very high temperatures up to $10^{10}$ K or with high energy particles that interact with matter or magnetic fields producing high energy photons, both in the X and $\gamma$ regions of the electromagnetic spectrum. 

The limiting factor to the detection of X-rays is detector sensitivity. Indeed, the incoming fluxes from astrophysical sources are quite small. For reference, during active phases the Sun can reach a flux of $10^6$ ph cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$ in the range 2-10 keV while Scorpius X-1 has a flux of about 30 ph cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$ and 3C 273, the most luminous AGN, about 0.01 ph cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$. The necessity to improve flux sensitivity, in order to discover fainter sources, has led the research and developement of X-ray detectors, which have evolved substantially across the years and have employed very different technologies.

\section{History of X-ray astrophysics}
The first orbiting satellites to employ instrumentation to observe X-rays were part of the Orbital Solar Observatory (OSO) program, but they were mainly focused on the study of the Sun X-ray emission. Despite their main mission goals, they also performed some observations of galactic and extragalactic sources that allowed to confirm discoveries made by other missions with sub-orbital flights. Without dedicated instrumentation it was not possible to carry on systematic studies of fainter sources. In this sense, a big step ahead was made with UHURU, the first orbital mission fully dedicated to X-ray observations of extrasolar sources, launched in 1970. Before UHURU, each sub-orbital flight was able to observe the sky for about five minutes, and during the entire 1960s only about one hour of observations was cumulatively gathered. With the launch of UHURU, observations were carried continuosly for over two years, collecting an amount of data many orders of magnitude larger than the entire previous decade. This satellite performed an all-sky survey, scanning a different region of the sky each day. The results allowed to discover more than 300 new sources \cite{4ucatalog}, localized with a precision of 1 arc min that permitted the identification of counterparts in other regions of the electromagnetic spectrum.

The heritage of UHURU was collected by the following missions part of the program of high-energy observatories for X and $\gamma$ astrophysics, the three High Energy Astronomy Observatories (HEAO). One of the most important among them is HEAO-2, known as Einstein Observatory, launched in 1978. Einstein is the first X-ray telescope, in the strict sense of the term, meaning that, for the first time, mirrors were used to focus X-rays onto a detector located in the focal point, marking the birth of imaging X-ray astronomy and paving the way to all the future detectors of this type. Until that moment, an X-ray observatory usually had a set of collimators to mechanically delimit the observed sky region. With different collimator geometries and with dedicated reconstruction algorithms it is possible to achieve a resolution of at most a few arc sec (about $7"$--$10"$ for HEAO-1 \cite{heao1a3}), but still it was not possible to produce images of extended sources. Moreover, for collimators a better spatial resolution implies a decrease of the effective area, with the consequent drop of the signal-to-noise ratio. With Einstein Observatory these problems were solved using focusing optics instead of collimators. However, while a mirror surface reflects almost all the incoming photons with in the visible band, high-energy photons have a very high probability of penetrating the surface and thus are not reflected. The solution to this problem is to use grazing incidence optics, relying on total reflection of light to have an increasing of reflectivity. This law holds for X-rays too, with the difference that the angle is usually below 1 degree and there is a quite strong dependence on the photon energy, making this method feasible only for low energy X-rays up to 10-20 keV. Wolter geometry is a possible optics geometry that consists in using two mirrors, usually one with parabolic section and one with hyperbolic or ellipsoid section, named after its designer Hans Wolter. This layout allows to have a field of view large enough for use in astrophysics and it is one of the reasons that makes it the most common layout between grazing incidence telescopes. The main problem of grazing incidence telescopes is the very small geometrical area due to the small inclination of the mirrors with respect to the optical axis. The natural solution to this problem is to use multiple nested concentrical mirrors to multiply the area, altough it introduces a series of technical and structural problematics that have been overcome in the years thanks to technological improvements. Einstein Observatory employed four nested mirrors with a diameter varying between 33 and 56 cm, a focal lenght of 3.4 m, a geometrical area of about 400 cm$^2$ and the capability of alternating four different detectors on the focal plane. For high resolution imaging, the effective area dropped down to 20 cm$^2$ at 0.25 keV and 5 cm$^2$ at 2 keV, with a spatial resolution of 2 arc sec \cite{heao2}. Other imaging and spectroscopy instruments on board were able to achieve an effective area between 100-200 cm$^2$. Even with a smaller effective area than HEAO-1 the results were remarkable because the first source X-ray images were produced, allowing to study their morphology in a completely new electromagnetic band. This was possible because, contrary to collimator observatories, the entire effective area was focused on a small region of the sky, reducing background signal and consequently increasing the signal-to-noise ratio and the instrumental sensitivity. Einstein Observatory produced a detailed catalogue with almost \num{5000} sources observing just a small fraction of the sky, and for most of them the localization was accurate enough to identify their optical counterparts. 

The successive step in the creation of a new all-sky catalogue was lead by ROSAT, launched in 1990. ROSAT had an overall design very similar to its predecessor Einstein Observatory, including the same grazing incidence optics layout but with larger and more efficient mirrors, that permitted to increase the effective area, and a different focal ratio to have a larger field of view to scan the entire sky. Indeed, ROSAT had a field of view twice as large as Einstein's largest one and it also had a more uniform sensitivity across all the field of view. In just 6 months, ROSAT was able to observe almost \num{150000} sources \cite{rosat}. During its nine years of operations, ROSAT also studied in detail many different sources, including emission from galactic stars and compact objects, spectral and morphological studies of supernova remnants. For the first time, it was also able to resolve the diffuse extragalactic emission into tens of thousands of AGN, giving a fundamental contribution to the development of the unified model and to variability studies. The limit of ROSAT was its limited energy range, which was partially attributable to the mirrors geometry, that had a relatively big angle of inclination with respect to the focal axis to achieve a large field of view sacrifying sensitivity to higher energies, but was also due to the detectors employed for reveal X-rays. Indeed, the main imaging detectors types used until ROSAT were Position Sensitive Proportional Counters (PSPC) and Micro-Channel Plates (MCP). PSPC, which is a gas detector, looses sensitivity at high energies because the gas becomes optically thin with a consequent reduction of the quantum efficiency. For similar reasons, MCPs are very unefficent at high energies, because X-rays easily penetrate channels' coating and glass. Furthermore, even though MCPs were the only way to achieve very good spatial resolutions, they are not capable to measure photon energy, requiring to alternate different instruments in the focal plane to study morphology and spectrum. 

To overcome these limitations a new mission was launched in 1993, Advanced Satellite for Cosmology and Astrophyisics (ASCA), with two revolutionary changes. The first difference was in the optics design, that for the first time in a space telescope were realized using a large number of nested metal foils coated with a high-Z metal. ASCA had four telescopes, each with 120 nested gold-coated foils, allowing to position them with a very small angle with respect to the optical axis, thus increasing both the effective area, which reached 325 cm$^2$ at 1 keV for each of them (when considering only the optics), and the observable energy range up to 12 keV. The second innovation consisted in 
the use of CCDs as main detectors in the focal plane, the first time for an X-ray telescope. Using CCDs in photon counting mode it became possible to perform high resolution imaging and spectroscopy of the source at the same time with a single instrument, even at high energies.

The first generation of X-ray CCDs suffered from various problems, such as low quantum efficiency at low and high energies, with the latter caused by a small depletion region and electronics effect were responsible for spectral resolution deterioration. These problems were solved by the following generation of X-ray observatories, with Chandra and XMM-Newton, both launched in 1999 and currently operational. These two missions pushed to the limit the technological advancement of CCDs. XMM-Newton is the first X-ray observatory to employ a high thickness and fully depleted CCD \cite{xmm_pn}, which ensures a high quantum efficiency in the entire operational energy range dictated by the optics, which reaches 15 keV. On the other hand, Chandra's CCDs were optimized for low energy X-ray photons and spatial resolution, improving the readout electronics to decrease noise and also reducing the pixel size, in order to take full advantage of the 10 meters long focal length and achieve a spatial resolution of $0.5$ arc sec \cite{chandra}. \fix{piccola frase conclusiva}

Beyond imaging and spectroscopy, polarization of the X-ray emission from a source can be studied to infer important properties about the environment surrounding it and the physical mechanisms at play, such as information about magnetic fields and source geometry \cite{xray_polarimetry}. The first successful X-ray polarimetry mission took place in 1971 and was carried out with sub-orbital rockets using a crystal Bragg  polarimeter, resulting in the first measure of the polarization level of the Crab Nebula. This type of detector consists in a crystal of a specific material, which defines a narrow energy sensitivity band, mounted at an angle of 45 degrees with respect to the incident flux. If a photon has the electric field vector parallel to the crystal surface it is reflected, otherwise it penetrates the crystal and gets absorbed. By counting the photons that gets reflected by the crystal as a function of the azimuthal angle while the detector is spinning around the axis that points towards the source, it is possible to measure the level of polarization. For many years this method represented the only feasible way to measure X-ray polarization, but there were many pysical and technical problems associated to it. First of all, the energy range is very narrow, thus the observed flux is small and the consequence is that even for bright sources the observation time required to perform the measures is very long. The second big problem was technical, because it is difficult to let the detector spin pointing exactly towards the source, with the possibility of introducing systematics errors that could false the polarization. For these reasons a mission completely oriented to X-ray polarimetry was never realized until a new technique to measure photon polarization was developed. This method consists in using the dependence on the polarization of the direction of motion of primary photoelectrons released in photoelectric absorption. This is the basis of the operation of Imaging X-ray Polarimetry Explorer (IXPE), launched in 2021, where Gas Pixel Detectors (GPD) are employed. IXPE allows to reach high sensitivity with observing times at least two orders of magnitude lower than old missions. Another important aspect of IXPE is its ability to provide not only polarization data of the observed source, but also imaging, spectral and timing data at the same time. More details about IXPE and its detectors will be given in Chap. \ref{chap:chapter3}.

It is fundamental to identify the main physical processes involved in the interaction of radiation with matter to correctly modelize the behavior of a detector and being able to simulate it and reconstruct the intresting quantities from the observed signals.

In this work, two different types of detectors have been employed, a gas-filled detector and a semiconductor detector. Even if the materials that are responsible for the conversion of photons into charges are very different, the underlying physical processes are the same. A short description of these processes is discussed in this chapter.

\paragraph{Photoelectric effect}
A photon can interact with an atom via t

\begin{equation}
\gamma + (A, Z) \rightarrow e^- + (A, Z)^+
\end{equation}

In order to interact with the photoelectric effect, the photon energy $E_{\gamma}$ must be higher than the binding energy $E_{B}$ of the atomic electron, whose kinetic energy after ejection is:

\begin{equation}
E = E_{\gamma} - E_{B}.
\label{eq:photoelectric_kinetic_e}
\end{equation} 

The cross section of this interaction process shows a strong dependence on the energy and the atomic number $Z$, and with increasing $Z$ the absorption edges can be observed. These edges are rapid increases in the cross section that occur when the photon has enough energy to interact with electrons inside more internal atomic shells. The trend of the cross section from the energy of the photon depends on which shell the photon is interacting with, but an approximated treatment of the interaction with K-shell electrons shows that it goes as

\begin{equation}
\sigma_{photo} \propto E^{-3.5}Z^5.
\end{equation}

Fig. \ref{fig:photo_cross_section} shows the photoelectric cross section of silicon and cadmium telluride (CdTe) as a function of the photon energy, and it is possible to see the absorption edges of the two materials. It is possible to see that the absorption cross section of a shell is dominant until the photon has an energy large enough to interact with an inner shell.

\end{figure}

The photoelectric effect is the dominant interaction process from energies lower than 1 keV up to tens of keV or even hundreds of keV for high-$Z$ elements.
