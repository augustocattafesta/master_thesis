\chapter{Analog Spectral Imager for X-rays}
\label{chap:chapter4}
\cite{leo1994} \cite{kolanoski2020} \cite{tomaiuolo2024}
The Analog Spectral Imager for X-rays (ASIX) is a new class of solid-state hybrid pixel detectors (HPDs), with the ability to perform single photon event-driven readout, designed for both imaging and spectral study of photons at X-ray energies between 1 keV and 50 keV. The intended applications for this detectors are X-ray spectral imaging, material science and also micro-tomography in various fields. 

In this section the detector design and the current state of the prototypes will be illustrated. Afterwards, the algorithms and the procedure to perform detector calibration are outlined, in order to allow the best event reconstruction possible, involving both position and energy reconstruction. In this regard, different possible algorithms for position reconstruction and their perfomance are shown, as well as the limits of the energy reconstruction. Then, a comparison between Monte Carlo simulations and real data is done, to verify the goodness of the simulation software to replicate the prototypes and to have a benchmark for the event reconstruction algorithms performance.


\section{Detector design}
\label{sec:asix_design}

ASIX is a solid-state HPD which consists of two main components: the pixelated semiconductor active sensor, which is responsible for the absorption of X-ray photons and the production of hole-electron pairs, and the analog readout ASIC, whose job is to process the collected charge by amplifying and shaping the signal and then triggering the digital readout electronics.

\subsection{Active sensor}
\label{subsec:sensor}
\cite{rossi2006}
The active sensor is the part of the detector where the physical signal is produced by interation mechanisms between radiation and the sensor material, which is ionized during the passage of charge particles or electromagnetic radiation. In solid state detectors the active sensor is made of a semiconductor material. There are various choices for the sensor material, depending on the required properties of the detector and the type or radiation that has to be detected. For ASIX, which works with X-rays, the relevant materials are silicon and cadmium-telluride (CdTe), which currently are the two type of prototypes available. As already discussed in Chap. \ref{chap:chapter2}, CdTe is best suited for the harder portion of the electromagnetic spectrum, as the higher atomic numbers of cadmium and tellurium imply a higher photoelectric cross section than silicon, especially above photon energies of 30 keV. At these energies the main interaction process is represented by the photoelectric effect, thus other processes can be neglected for the discussion.

The probability density function of a photon of being absorbed by the sensor material at a depth $z$ is described by Eq. \ref{eq:absorption}, where $\mu$ is the attenuation factor, which can be calculated for a given energy knowing the cross section and the density of the material. This equation allows to calculate the probability of being absorbed by the sensor, which can define the sensitivity energy range of the detector given the sensor material and its thickness. Another important quantity that can be calculated from Eq. \ref{eq:absorption} is the mean absorption depth as a function of the energy, shown in Fig. \ref{fig:mean_z}, which is

\begin{equation}
\langle z \rangle = \int_0^{d} \frac{\mu z e^{- \mu z}}{1 - e^{-\mu d}} = \frac{1 - e^{-\mu d} (1 + \mu d)}{\mu (1 - e^{-\mu d})}.
\label{eq:mean_z}
\end{equation}

The jumps that can be observed are effects caused by the K-edges. When the cross section becomes too small at high energies the mean value goes asymptotically to half the sensor thickness because the interaction probability becomes uniform. The right axis shows the absorption probability as a function of the energy for the same sensors.}
    \label{fig:mean_z}
\end{figure}

\fix{forse questo va nel chap 2, oppure descritto per ixpe e ref qui} The interaction between the photon and the sensor initially produces a single free electron, which ionizes other atoms by collisions producing secondary electrons, and an excited atom, which can return to the ground state with two different mechanisms. The first is by releasing an Auger electron from the outer shell with enough energy to ionize other atoms. The second mechanism consists in producing fluorescence X-ray radiation by emitting an X-ray from the transition of an electron from outer shells into the inner shell of the electron emitted by photoelectric effect. This second X-ray photon can interact with the sensor producing another primary electron or can also escape from the detector, which can lead to the appearance of an escape peak, depending on the detector geometry. The number of electron-hole pairs is given by Eq. \ref{eq:num_ioniz} and depends on the sensor material. For semiconductors $w$ is smaller than for gases, thus the number of electrons produced by a photon is bigger for solid-state detectors. This property is essential to have a better energy resolution.

This discussion is valid for any piece of material subjected to incoming radiation. In order to have an active sensor usable in a detector it has to satisfy different requierments, above all it must be made of an opportunely doped semiconductor in order to create a junction. The working principles of a semiconductor junction have already been explained in Chap. \ref{sec:semiconductors}. Currently there are two different ASIX prototypes \cite{minuti2025}: an hexagonally pixelated n-on-p 300 $\mu$m thick silicon sensor with a pixel pitch of 50 $\mu$m, and an hexagonally pixelated Schottky-type 750 $\mu$m thick cadmium-telluride sensor, shown in Fig. \ref{fig:cdte_proto}, with a pixel pitch of 100 $\mu$m. With the current ASIC readout chip, which has hexagonal pixels with 50 $\mu$m pitch, the CdTe sensor requires to connect one out of every four readout pixels.

Besides defining the sensitive energy range of the detector, the sensor material, thickness and working conditions also define how the collected charge diffuses inside it. Once electron-hole pairs are produced by the photon interaction and consequent collision ionizations, which happen in a very short region about 1 $\mu$m wide, approximable as point-like, they start to drift towards opposite poles of the junction under the action of the electric field. Thermal agitation and collisions with the crystal lattice make the charge spread durign the drift in the direction transverse to it. This process is governed by Fick's diffusion laws and in absence of external fields, which is true for the transverse, the charge is distributed on the plane according to a two-dimensional Gaussian distribution, with standard deviation along the $x$ and $y$ axes given by

\begin{equation}
\sigma = \sigma_x = \sigma_y = \sqrt{2Dt_d},
\label{eq:diff_sigma}
\end{equation}
where $D$ is the diffusion coefficient and $t_d$ is the drift time. Using the mobility of the charge carriers $\mu_c$ and the value of the electric field $\mathcal{E}$ it is possible to make explicit the dependence of Eq. \ref{eq:diff_sigma} on the drift distance $d$

\begin{equation}
\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{2D}{\mu_c \mathcal{E}}d} = \sqrt{\frac{2kT}{e\mathcal{E}}d}= \mathcal{D} \sqrt{d},
\label{eq:diff_sigma_dist}
\end{equation}
where it has been used that in a semiconductor $D/\mu_c = kT/e$ and the transverse diffusion parameter $\mathcal{D}$ has been defined to isolate the dependence on the drift distance $d$. As clearly visible in Eq. \ref{eq:diff_sigma_dist}, there is also a dependence on the sensor working conditions, specifically the temperature and the electric field. This has to be taken into account during the analysis, because a wrong estimate of $\mathcal{D}$ can lead to errors, as will be clear in the following sections. The mean charge diffusion standard deviation can be calculated from Eqs. \ref{eq:mean_z} and \ref{eq:diff_sigma_dist}, and is useful to have an idea of how the charge cloud dimension varies as a function of the energy

\begin{equation}
\langle \sigma \rangle = \mathcal{D} \sqrt{t - \langle z \rangle},
\label{eq:mean_diff_sigma}
\end{equation}
where $t$ is the sensor thickness. Fig. \ref{fig:mean_sigma} shows $\langle \sigma \rangle$ as a function of the photon energy for the two sensors.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter4/design/mean_sigma}
    \caption{Normalized mean diffusion sigma as a function of the photon energy for the Si and CdTe sensors. In the sensitive energy range of the two sensors (up to about 15 keV for Si and 100 keV for CdTe) the average charge cloud size decreases of about 25\%.}
    \label{fig:mean_sigma}
\end{figure}

Once the sensor working conditions like the temperature and the electric field are chosen, meaning that $\mathcal{D}$ is determined, Eq. \ref{eq:mean_diff_sigma} allows to find the best values of the sensor thickness and pixel pitch depending on the main intent of the detector. If the goal is an imaging detector, the best choice would be to produce a large charge diffusion cloud in order to hit a lot of pixels and then reconstruct the position even with low effort algorithms. Conversely, for a spectroscope the smaller the number of pixels involved in the event, the better the energy resolution is, as the total readout noise on the final measurement goes as $\sqrt{N}$, where $N$ is the number of pixels. ASIX's goal is to find a compromise between an imager detector and a spectroscope, and thanks to a low noise readout and the pixel size and geometry, good performances for both tasks are achievable. For this reason the sensor thickness and pixel pitch are chosen in order to have a ratio between the charge diffusion sigma and the pixel pitch in the range between 10-20\%. The relevant metric to gauge how this ratio affects the detector readout design and the event analysis is the cluster size, which is the number of pixels that have signal above a certain threshold. For a ratio of about 15\% the distribution becomes symmetric, and is expected to become skewed towards small cluster sizes below this ratio.

\subsection{Readout}
\label{subsec:readout}
The goal of ASIX to be a detector suitable for different application fields under various working conditions makes it necessary to have high-performance readout electronics. One of the property which is particularly important is the readout rate, which should be high enough to be able to work in environments with high intensity sources. The current design goal is to have a maximum rate capability of 100 kHz with a 30\% deadtime and a readout frequency of 20 MHz. Another important property, which is essential to do high resolution spectroscopy, is the equivalent noise charge introduced by the electronics. The goal is to have no more than 20 ENC for each pixel and a final energy resolution of about 350 eV at the energy of 8 keV when using the silicon sensor, which is enough to resolve the Cu-K$_{\alpha}$ and Cu-K$_{\beta}$ lines. All of this is achievable with a dedicated analog front-end ASIC. The proposed ASIC design employs the same 180 nm CMOS fabrication process used for XPOL-III, with 50 $\mu$m pitch hexagonal pixels arranged with a honeycomb layout over a rectangular matrix, with a design goal dimension up to $31 \times 25$ mm$^2$ with more than $\num{350000}$ pixels. Unlike in GPDs, where the readout pixels are physically separated from the GEM, in the ASIX architecture they are directly connected to the pixelated sensor through micro-bump soldering, aligning each ASIC pixel to the corresponding one on the sensor. Like XPOL-III, each pixel has its own analog signal processing electronics, with a charge-sensitive amplifier, a shaping circuit and a sample-and-hold system. Thus, the general structure of the ASIC is very similar to the previously developed XPOL-III, apart from some aspects to modify to achieve the goal designs mentioned above.

The important aspect that drives one of the major difference between XPOL and ASIX is the event topology of the detector. While in GPDs the electrons tracks produced by X-ray absorption can be tens of pixels long and can significantly vary, as discussed in \fix{Chap. \ref{chap:chapter3}}, in the proposed design of ASIX the number of pixels that are involved in an event is small, with an average of about two pixels and always less than eight pixels when applying a suppression threshold, and with an axial symmetrical charge distribution around the impact point. This event geometry is translated into an event that is fully contained by a region composed by a central pixel, which is the pixel with the most charge collected, and the six pixels that surround it. The limited dimension of the event and its fixed geometry  make possible to modify the trigger logic and the event readout strategy of the previous XPOL versions. The new chip is designed to be triggered by a single pixel, which is the one with the highest signal. The following readout consists in reading the pixel and all of its six neighbors, independently from their signal. The true advantage of this event geometry is the possibility to perform a parallel readout of the seven pixels using an external ADC with seven independent channels, with the outcome of drastically reducing the readout time, thus increasing the maximum acceptable rate of events. This can be done connecting each pixel to a single ADC channel, and the hexagonal honeycomb arrangement is ideal for this scope as it allows to map the entire chip with seven channels with no adjacent repetitions of the same channel, thus making possible to read a seven-pixels cluster on every point of the chip. A possible configuration of the ADC routing is shown in Fig. \ref{fig:adc_routing}.

\begin{figure}

The seven darker pixels represent an example of readout after the pixel connected to channel 3 has triggered the event.}
    \label{fig:adc_routing}

Another improvement of the rate capability can be achieved dividing the chip into independent regions, each with its own multi-channel ADC. This configuration with independent regions would cause problems for events at the borders of the region, because they would be truncated and thus they should be rejected, but with an optimal shape and size of the regions the fraction of events should be smaller than 1\%. 

To not exclude other possible uses of the chip other than for ASIX, such as for X-ray polarimetry, it is designed to support additional triggering and readout logics similar to XPOL-III, with a sequential readout of the pixels inside a rectangular ROI, as already described in Chap. \ref{sec:xpol3}. This would still allow to analyze larger tracks, but obviously the speed is slower and the maximum rate capability is smaller.

\section{Simulations and analysis framework}
To estimate the properties of different detector designs prior to the realization and then to characterize a prototype, a framework to generate Monte Carlo simulations of the detector and analyze the  data has been developed \cite{hexsample}. 

In this section a description of the Monte Carlo simulation software and the analysis pipeline are given.

\subsection{Monte Carlo simulations}
Monte Carlo simulations of the detector are essential to understand its capabilities depending on the design. As mentioned in the previous section, designing a detector which has both imaging and spectroscopical high-resolution capabilities requires to carefully tune the active sensor dimensions to achieve the correct trade-off between the two. With the first prototypes it is also possible to validate the simulations with laboratory data, and if necessary finely tweaking the Monte Carlo to match the data, in order to have a software that can reliably simulate the real detector. The simulation consists of different steps, each representing a different stage of the detector.

First, it is necessary to define the detector design, specifying the active sensor and the readout chip properties. The active sensor is described by three parameters, which are the material, the thickness and the transverse diffusion coefficient of the electrons, defined in Eq. \ref{eq:diff_sigma_dist} and that depends on the working conditions. The readout chip is described by the pixel pitch, the number of columns and rows and the readout logic, which allows to choose between the new chip seven-pixels parallel readout and the XPOL-III sequential rectangular readout with the additional option to choose the padding dimensions. The readout chip is also characterized by three other quantities, the ENC, the pedestal and the gain, which are provided in form of matrices with the same dimension of the chip to take into account spatial variations among the chip. The last thing to setup is the X-ray source, which is described by its energy spectrum and the beam shape, position and spatial distribution.

In the simulation, a photon with a given energy is sampled from the source spectrum and then its absorption depth is extracted from \fix{Eq. \ref{}}. It is assumed that photons are perpendicularly incident on the sensor surface and that all the electrons are produced in a point-like region located in the absorption point. The diffusion is modeled as discussed in Chap. \ref{subsec:sensor}, assuming a two-dimensional Gaussian distribution centered on the impact point and with a standard deviation which is determined by the absorption depth. Afterwards, the event readout is performed according to the chosen logic.

The simulation does not take into account some physical effects, such as the distance travelled by primary photo-electrons or the possibility of having a fluorescence photon that can travel through the sensor before being absorbed, with the consequence that a point-like conversion region is no more a good approximation. The first effect is relevant at high energies for both Si and CdTe sensors, as shown in Fig. \ref{fig:range_si_cdte}, as an electron with an energy below 10 keV stops in less than 1 $\mu$m, thus the validity of the approximation gets worse with increasing energy. The second effect depends both on energy and the sensor material, as the probability of producing a fluorescence photon, called fluorescence yield, depends on the involved shell and on the atomic number $Z$. For silicon the probability is less than 5\% for K-shell and almost 0\% for L-shell, while for cadmium and tellurium is between 83-88\% for K-shell and about 5-7\% for L-shell. If the photon is produced, given that the emission angle distribution is isotropic, it travels through the sensor until it is absorbed and produces a new photo-electron. Depending on the photon energy, the new photo-electron can be produced even a pixel pitch far from the first electron. For CdTe sensor this drastically decreases the simulation accuracy, but only for energies above the K-edge, which is about 27 keV. \fix{All of this could be solved implementing the abosoprtion simulation with common physical simulation software.} Another effect which is not taken into account is pixel crosstalk, which is responsible for charge sharing between pixels. A simple way to partially solve this is to include this effect into the charge diffusion process, increasing the diffusion parameter calculated from Eq. \ref{eq:diff_sigma_dist}. The correct value of this parameter can be chosen comparing the cluster size distribution between laboratory data and simulation until they match.

\subsection{Analysis}
Simulated and laboratory data can then be analyzed to extract the relevant information. Raw signal data consists of a set of PHA values, one for each pixel inside the region of interest defined by the readout logic. Raw PHA values need to be corrected for two contributions, the pedestal and pixel equalization, to get the correct PHA value for each pixel. \fix{As mentioned in the previous section}, the pedestal subtraction may be performed offline, so a map of it is necessary to process all the pixels. Pixel equalization, instead, takes into account possible variations between pixels that may be introduced by the electronics \fix{(These may be corrected directly from the chip,ask)} and spatial inhomogeneities in the sensor, and a map is necessary for this correction too. Specific datasets are required to produce these maps, and the process to produce them is discussed in the Chap. \ref{sec:calibration}.

The corrected PHA values can be used to analyze the event and reconstruct photon properties, such as energy and impact position. According to the algorithm used for the reconstruction, an additional processing can be performed. In some cases all the seven PHA values are necessary, while in others a further reduction of the pixels can be made. Particularly, as shown in Tab. \ref{tab:charge_distr}, about 99\% of all the charge is collected by the three pixels with most signal. This information can be used to create a cluster containing only pixels that have a high probability of having collected signal. Specifically, the first step is to choose a zero suppression threshold, expressed in terms of noise standard deviation, to suppress all the pixels that are dominated by noise. Given that each pixel can have a different noise distribution, a map of this quantity is also necessary. The second step uses a geometrical constraint, imposing that all the pixels above threshold must be adjacent. The choice of which pixels are suppressed is made by starting from the outer pixel with the highest signal, and then going into decreasing order: if the following pixel is not its neighbour it is suppressed. This is done until a maximum size of three neighbors pixels above threshold, for the reasons stated above. The consequence of this suppression is that the cluster size is constrained to be between one and three.


\section{Calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}
To correctly reconstruct events' properties, such as the energy and the incident position of the photons, it is first necessary to calibrate the readout chip, to compensate for noise, pedestal and response variations between the pixels. This step is essential to maximize the detector performance, achieving the best spatial and energy resolution possible.

Overall, there are five calibration files that can be created associated to the following quantities: equivalent noise charge (ENC), ADC noise, pedestal, pixel equalization, gain. Some of these files can be obtained directly analyzing event files, while others are calculated as a combination of the others. Furthermore, some are essential for event reconstruction to correct non-uniformity, while others are only relevant for simulation purposes.  

In the analysis pipeline, the calibration of noise and pedestal is performed together and is the first step. Pixels' equalization calibration is then performed using the first two calibration results. A file containing all the information about the calibrated quantity is produced. The essential products inside this file are three matrices with the same number of columns and rows as the readout chip:
\begin{itemize}[label=-]
\item \textit{values}, containing the central value of the calibrated quantity for each pixel;
\item \textit{entries}, containing the number of events used for the calibration for each pixel;
\item \textit{errors}, the estimated uncertainties associated to the value of the calibrated quantity for each pixel.
\end{itemize}

The possible methods to calibrate these quantities are illustrated in this section, along with the results obtained from the simulations.


\subsection{Noise and pedestal}
\label{sec:noise_calibration}

Noise and pedestal calibrations require data acquisitions without any source of radiation or, alternatively, it is possible to use acquisitions with a source on by not considering pixels in the readout regions that may have collected some source signal. In this work, given the avalaiblity of only data acquisitions with the source on, only the latter case will be discussed and used.

The typical region of interest of an event corresponds to a rectangle with a width of ten pixels and a height of 13 pixels, with a total of 130 pixels. It may be possible that more than one mini-cluster is triggered, in which case the number of pixels in the region of interest increases, depending on the number of mini-clusters. To minimize the probability of using pixels with source signal all the pixels in the region of trigger and their neighbors are excluded from the analysis, taking the number of good pixels for the analysis to 121 on average. Fig. \ref{fig:noise_pedestal_roi} shows an example of event readout.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/chapter4/calibration/roi}
    \caption{Example of the region of interest of an event with the source on. Central pixels with marked borders belongs to the region of trigger and are excluded from the calibration analysis, together with all their neighbors. This image is taken from a simulation with pixel noise and pedestals distributed according to Gaussian distributions with 20 ADC and 1000 ADC, respectively, and 10\% standard deviation.}
    \label{fig:noise_pedestal_roi}
\end{figure}

To estimate the values of noise and pedestal of a pixel is necessary to sample its PHA distribution, whose  shape depends on the working conditions and readout chip properties. If the pedestal is bigger than the noise, the resulting distribution is a Gaussian distribution with mean and standard deviation corresponding to the pedestal and noise, respectively. Otherwise, if the pedestal is small with respect to the noise, the resulting distribution is still a Gaussian distribution but with a truncated negative part. In the latter case, the mean and the standard deviations of the sample distribution do not represents the pedestal and noise.

According to the working conditions, different methods to calibrate the readout chip can be applied. All of them are easily implementable, but choosing the correct one can improve accuracy, numerical stability or computational time needed when calibrating the entire chip.

The first method that can always be used is to perform a least-squares minimization using a Gaussian as the fit model. This method does not need any correction, but it requires a lot of computation resources to fit the entire chip. Furthermore, it's not stable when fitting half-truncated distributions, making difficult to find the correct valeus of pedestal and noise. [WE NEED TO CONFIRM THIS WITH SOME SIMULATIONS]

The second method consists of calculating the mean and the standard deviation of the sample distribution. This works only when the distribution is not truncated, so it is possible to use it only when the pedestal is much greater than the noise, so that at most just a small fraction of the left tail is truncated. Contrary to the first method, this one is numerically very stable and fast.

The last method is a special case of the second one that can only be applied when the pedestal is negligible with respect to the noise and it can be assumed to be 0 ADC. In this case, the sample distribution is a half-normal distribution defined only on the positive axis, whose probability density function is

\begin{equation}
p(x; \sigma^2) = 
\begin{cases} 
\frac{2}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^2}} \exp \left( -\frac{x^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) & \text{if } x \ge 0, \\ 
0 & \text{if } x < 0.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:half_normal}
\end{equation}

It can be easily shown that the variance of this distribution is equal to the variance of the full parent Gaussian, which is the quantity that we want to estimate, using the parity of the Gaussian distribution. We thus recover the possibility to estimate it as the sample variance of the observed distribution.

Regarding the uncertainties on the estimated quantities for the last two methods, the error associated to the pedestal $\hat{p}$ is the uncertainty of the sample mean, and for the noise $\hat{n}$ is the one associated to the sample standard deviation, thus

\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\hat{p}} = \frac{\hat{n}}{\sqrt{N}}, \quad \quad \sigma_{\hat{n}} = \frac{\hat{n}}{\sqrt{2N}},
\label{eq:noise_uncertainty}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the number of events collected for the pixel. 


\subsection{Pixel equalization}

The goodness of the estimate of the energy and the position of a photon is particularly sensible to variations in pixel response across the chip, thus it is necessary to compensate these variations to increase the detector performances at the best of its capacity.

To calibrate this quantity it is necessary to illuminate the chip with a source. In principle, there is no constraint on the source spectrum, but the best results are achievable with a monochromatic source, as it will be shown later. The calibration can be performed in two different ways, depending on the knowledge of the incoming spectrum and the ability to accurately reproduce it in the simulations.

The first method does not require any previous knowledge of the spectrum, as it only uses one-pixel events. The idea is that the PHA distribution of one-pixel events, which are events where all the charge is deposited in a single pixel, have the same shape across all the chip pixels and only their location varies. Thus, the estimated sample mean can be used to equalize the pixels by calculating the mean of the sample mean distributions and constraining it to be equal to 1. To identify one-pixel events it is necessary apply a zero suppression of the pixels after choosing a threshold, which may be expressed as numbers of noise sigma associated to the pixels. As discussed in Chap. \ref{sec:asix_design}, there is some arbitrarity in the choice of the threshold, but if the chosen value is zero or is too low the number ofgood events for each pixel is too small, hence without a sufficiently large statistics the estimate of the mean is not accurate. If the threshold is too high the spectrum does not resemble the source spectrum, but ... The second method is based on the modeling of the source spectrum, which can be used as a probability density function to calculate the likelihood of the total charge collected by an event, which is dependent on the equalization factors of the pixels involved in the event. Contrary to the first method, this method requires to exaclty know the source spectrum and being able to simulate the spectrum for the fully calibrated and equalized detector. This means that is first necessary to run the noise and pedestal calibration, and it should be possible to convert the ADC noise values to ENC values, in order to run the simulation. \fix{(FARE CHIAREZZA IN QUESTO PASSAGGIO, PERCHè CI DOVREBBE VOLERE UN PRIMO RUN DEL PRIMO METODO)}. After a spectrum dataset is simulated, the event reconstruction can performed, and the resulting ADC values distribution has to be transformed into a probability density function. This operation is performed using gaussian kernel density estimation (KDE). As the spectrum \textit{pdf} has to be called at each evaluation of the likelihood and the KDE is a slow algorithm, it is once calculated for a large grid of values and then interpolated with a cubic spline to speed up the calculation. Both these steps are performed using functions implemented in scientific analysis libraries, such as \cite{scipy2020}.

After having calculated the probability density function $p(E)$ of the source spectrum, it is possible to calculate the likelihood of a single event, which is defined as

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(p_1, ..., p_n) = p \left( \sum_{i=1}^n q_i p_i \right),
\label{eq:equalization_likelihood}
\end{equation}

where $n$ is the number of pixel involved in a single event, $q_i$ is the ADC counts read by the $i$-th pixel and $p_i$ is the inverse of the equalization factor. The choice of fitting the inverse of the equalization factor is due to stability in the fit. As clearly visible, this method allows to include all the pixels above the chosen threshold in all events, without discriminating between the cluster sizes, guaranteeing a much larger statistics than the previous method, hence accuracy and stability. In theory it could be possible to calculate the total likelihood for all the $N$ events across all the $n_{pix}$ pixels of the chip

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(p_1, ..., p_{n_{pix}}) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} p \left( \sum_{j=1}^{n_{pix}} q_j p_j \right),
\label{eq:total_likelihood}
\end{equation}

and minimize it with respect to all the fit parameters. In practice, this is not feasible due to the large number of parameters, more than $10^5$, requiring too much space in the memory during the fit. For this reason the chip is divided into smaller square sub-regions and all the pixels inside the same sub-region are fitted simultaneously. To be considered as an event belonging to the sub-region, all the charge must be collected only by pixels inside it, thus pixels that lie on the borders can loose part of the events when they share the charge with pixels that are outside it. As a consequence, sub-regions have a minimum overlapping of two columns and rows in order that pixels that are on the borders for one of them are not on the border for the successive one. For each sub-region the negative log-likelihood is calculated from Eq. \ref{eq:total_likelihood} and then minimized with \textit{iminuit} \cite{iminuit}.

\section{Position reconstruction}
Once the detector is fully calibrated, it is possible to correctly reconstruct all the information about the events. The important thing to take into account is that a feature of this detector is that the size of the diffused charge cloud is a bit smaller than the pixels, meaning that the average cluster size is quite small, as already seen in \ref{sec:asix_design}. While this feature is essential for spectroscopy in order to minimize the contribution of noise onto the energy estimate, it introduces the need of a careful analysis to reconstruct the incident position of photons. As a consequence of this, different algorithms have been developed and compared to find which one is able to reconstruct incident positions with the best resolution possible. In this section, the algorithms are described and compared. Two methods have been used to estimate the resolution achieved by each algorithm: the first one is based on Monte Carlo truth from the simulations; the second one consists of studying the spatial response of the detector to a slit or a sharp edge. Furthermore, the dependance of the spatial resolution on noise and on the cluster size is shown.

\subsection{Charge barycenter}
\label{subsec:barycenter}
The first algorithm is the most simple one. It consists of calculating the barycenter of the pixels' centers, each weighted by the charge collected

\begin{equation}
x = \frac{\sum_i x_i \cdot q_i}{\sum_i q_i}, \quad y = \frac{\sum_i y_i \cdot q_i}{\sum_i q_i}.
\end{equation}

This method implicitly assumes that the charge in each pixel is spread uniformly across it or, similarly, is concentrated in its center, and it does not consider how the charge diffuses inside the sensor. In the case where the charge would spread on a large number of pixels, this assumption would be valid and the charge barycenter would be a good estimator of the position. As mentioned before, this detector is not the case, as the cluster size is very small, thus only few pixels collect charge.

It must be noted that for one-pixel events, this is the only possible method that can be applied, as there is not enough information to reconstruct a position which is different from the pixel center. From this point on, it is assumed that every time a one-pixel event position is reconstructed, it corresponds to the pixel center. While this type of events has the best energy resolution, as will be shown in the next sections, it has the worst spatial resolution.

Fig. \ref{fig:barycenter_dx_dy} shows the residuals distributions for the $x$ and $y$ components between the reconstructed and the Monte Carlo truth positions. It is clearly visible how the pixel hexagonal geometry affects the reconstruction, in particular two-pixels and three-pixels events. For one-pixel events there is not much that can be done. The overall distributions are not gaussians and are quite broad.

The encircled energy fraction (EEF), which is the cumulative density function of the radial distance distribution, can be used to gauge the resolution of the detector \fix{[qualche cit, Fraser, G. W. (1989). X-ray Detectors in Astronomy, forse, da scaricare]}. Usually, the EEF is calculated from the point spread function (PSF), which is the response of a detector to a point source. In this case for the simulations, given the availability of the Monte Carlo truth positions, it can be calculated from the radial residuals distribution. Some common metrics of the EEF are the points where it is equal to 0.5 or 0.9, with the first value representing the distance at which 50\% of events are reconstructed, and it measures the "core" resolution, while the second value is more sensitive to the tails. Fig. \ref{fig:barycenter_eef} shows how the EEF of the barycenter algorithm varies as a function of the ratio between the dimension of the charge cloud and the pixel pitch. It can be observed that a larger charge cloud has an overall better resolution than smaller clouds, even if the current clustering algorithm only accepts at most three pixels in a cluster, meaning that part of the signal collected by other pixels could have been lost, with a consequent resolution deterioration.

\subsection{Likelihood estimator}
\label{subsec:mle}

The previous algorithm totally ignores how the charge diffuses inside the sensor, as it assumes that it uniformly spreads across the involved pixels. In \fix{Sec. \ref{}} it has been seen that inside the sensor the charge diffusion can be modeled with a bivariate Gaussian distribution centered on the incident position and with a standard deviation $\sigma$ that depends on the absorption depth, as well as the working conditions of the sensor. Considering the normalized charge distribution, the integral of the two-dimensional distribution over the pixel $P_i$ represents the charge fraction $f_i$ that is collected by it

\begin{equation}
f_i (x_0, y_0) = \int_{P_i} \frac{1}{2\pi \sigma^2} \exp \left[ - \frac{(x - x_0)^2 + (y - y_0)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right] \,dx\,dy,
\label{eq:mle_charge_fraction}
\end{equation}

If $Q = \sum_{i=0}^6 q_i$ is the total charge collected by all the seven pixels of the cluster, in case the noise is negligible, and the counts fluctuations are exclusively caused by statistical fluctuations, the single pixel charges are distributed according to a multinomial distribution, and the likelihood of the event is the following

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(x, y) = \frac{Q!}{\prod_i q_i!} \prod_i f_i(x,y) ^{q_i}.
\label{eq:mle_multinomial}
\end{equation}

In presence of electronic noise, this model does no longer holds, specially in high-noise conditions. To give an example, if a 8 keV photons is absorbed by a silicon sensor, about 2000 hole-electron pairs are produced. Assuming that the central pixel of the cluster collects $f_0 =$40\% of the charge, the associated statistical variance is

\begin{equation}
\sigma_s^2 = Q f_0 (1 - f_0) = 480 \sim \sigma_n^2 = 400,
\end{equation}

assuming that the detector as 20 ENC of noise. Therefore, the statistical variance is of the same order of the pixel electronic noise variance. For pixels that collect a small fraction of the charge the situation is even worse, because the electronic noise overwhelms statistical fluctuations, thus $\sigma_s^2 < \sigma_n^2$.

To correct this model and adapt it to consider the electronic noise, so that it can be used even in high-noise conditions, it is necessary to consider how the noise affects the observations. As seen in Sec. \ref{sec:noise_calibration}, the noise can be modeled as distributed according to a Gaussian distribution. As a consequence of this, each pixel charge distribution can be modeled with a Gaussian distribution for the following reasons:

\begin{itemize}[label=-]
\item if a pixel collects a lot of charge, the multinomial distribution converges to a Gaussian distribution for the central limit theorem. When the noise is added, the resulting variable is still distributed according to a Gaussian distribution whose variance is $\sigma^2 = \sigma^2_s + \sigma^2_n$;

\item if a pixel collects a small fraction of charge, its charge distribution follows a binomial distribution with a very low probability parameter $p$. This distribution is highly asymmetrical, peaked near zero and with a very low variance $\sigma^2_s$, negligible with respect to the electronic noise variance $\sigma^2_n$. This means that when summing the two variables, the noise dominates the signal charge, with the resulting distribution being a Gaussian distribution, whose variance can still be modeled as the sum of the two independent components $\sigma^2 = \sigma^2_s + \sigma^2_n$.
\end{itemize}

Therefore, as a first approximation, the charge probability density function of a single pixel is

\begin{equation}
p(q_i; \mu = f_i Q , \sigma^2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma} \exp \left[- \frac{(q_i - f_i Q)^2}{2\sigma} \right],
\label{eq:pdf_mle}
\end{equation}

where $\sigma$ is the standard deviation obtained from the squared sum of the two fluctuation components. 
Eq. \ref{eq:pdf_mle} lacks an important characteristic which is included in Eq. \ref{eq:mle_multinomial}, which is the correlation between the pixels. Indeed, this probability density function does not take into account the fact that if the incident position varies a bit, some pixels loose some of the charge, while others increase it. Thus, the statement that the pixels are independent can not be made. To take this aspect into account, the model has to be slightly modified. The correct probability density function is a generic multivariate Gaussian distribution

\begin{equation}
p(\vec{q}; x, y, Q) = \frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{7/2} |V|^{1/2}} \exp{\left[ -\frac{1}{2}(\vec{q} - \vec{f}Q)^{T} V^{-1} (\vec{q} - \vec{f}Q) \right]},
\label{eq:multivariate_gauss}
\end{equation}
where $\vec{q}$ is the seven-dimensional vector with all the cluster pixels charge observations, $\vec{f}$ is the vector with the charge fraction values, and $V$ is the covariance matrix, whose diagonal elements are the squared sums of the multinomial variances and noise fluctuations and out-diagonal elements are the covariances obtained from the multinomial model, which describe the charge sharing mechanism of the cloud

\begin{equation}
V_{ii} = \sigma^2_{s,i} + \sigma^2_{n,i}, \quad \quad V_{ij} = - Q f_i f_j \quad \textrm{for } i \neq j.
\label{eq:cov_matrix}
\end{equation}

In Eqns. \ref{eq:multivariate_gauss} and \ref{eq:cov_matrix}, the dependance from the incident position is hidden inside $\vec{f}$, so both the mean vector and the covariance matrix depend on the unknown parameters. At this point, the negative log-likelihood can be computed from Eq. \ref{eq:multivariate_gauss}

\begin{equation}
\operatorname{NLL}(x, y, Q) = -\ln \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}(\vec{q} - \vec{f}Q)^{T} V^{-1} (\vec{q} - \vec{f}Q) +  \frac{1}{2} \ln |V| + \textrm{const.}
\label{eq:nll_multivariate}
\end{equation}

This equation can now be minimized with respect to the unknown parameters $(x, y, Q)$ to find the best-fit incident position and total charge of the event. The minimization is performed with \cite{iminuit}.

\end{figure}

In order to be correctly used, this position reconstruction method requires a calibration. Indeed, the charge fractions depend on the standard deviation of the charge cloud distribution. In theory, they could be calculated with Eq. \ref{eq:mle_charge_fraction}, but the hexagonal geometry of the pixels makes the analytical calculation complex. For this reason, the computation is made using the Monte Carlo simulation software described in \fix{Sec. \ref{}}, using a specific set of source and detector parameters. During the fit, the minimizer evaluates the $\operatorname{NLL}$ for various pairs of $(x, y)$ coordinates. Performing this process on discrete matrices could lead to problems in the minimization, due to the possibility of having non-physical zero gradients. To avoid these problems, in the evaluation of the $\operatorname{NLL}$ there is a bilinear interpolation step, that allows to smooth the function. The interpolation is performed as following:

\begin{itemize}[label=-]
\item calculate the fractional coordinates $(w_x, w_y)$ of $(x, y)$ with respect to the associated bin center and get the grid coordinates $(i_x, i_y)$ of the nearest lower-left bin;
\item get the values $(v_{00}, v_{10}, v_{01}, v_{11})$ of the four bins with grid coordinates $(i_x, i_y)$, $(i_x + 1, i_y)$, $(i_x, i_y + 1)$, $(i_x + 1, i_y + 1)$;
\item weight each bin value with the distance from $(x, y)$ to get the interpolated value:
\begin{equation}
f_i (x, y) = v_{00} (1 - w_x) (1 - w_y) + v_{10} w_x (1 - w_y) + v_{01} (1 - w_x) w_y + v_{11} w_x w_y.
\end{equation}
\end{itemize}

To speed up the minimizer, the gradient of the $\operatorname{NLL}$ with resepect to $(x, y, Q)$ can be pre-computed. For this purpose, the gradients of the charge fraction matrices have to be computed with respect to $x$ and $y$ and have to be interpolated. This time the interpolation is linear, and the interpolated gradients are

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x} = [(v_{10} - v_{00}) (1 - w_y) + (v_{11} - v_{01}) w_y] / dx,\\
\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial y} = [(v_{01} - v_{00}) (1 - w_x) + (v_{11} - v_{10}) w_x] / dy,
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $dx = dy$ is the square bin size. \fix{The full computation of the NLL gradient is shown in App. \ref{app:A}.}

Contrary to the charge barycenter reconstruction, where the clustering algorithm uses a zero-suppression to cut pixels with few charge and also a position and cluster size suppression, where the maximum cluster size is fixed to three, so at most three neighbors pixels are taken to form a cluster, for this algorithm none of these suppressions are made. All seven pixels are kept for the reconstruction, so defining the cluster size becomes ambiguos. Just to compare the reconstruction results between the two methods, the cluster size of an event given by the charge barycenter algorithm is used also to categorize the same event reconstructed by the maximum likelihood estimator. Fig. \ref{fig:mle_dx_dy} shows the residuals distributions for the $x$ and $y$ position components. This time residuals distributions are regular and univariate. Moreover, the width of the distribution is much smaller than the previous algorithm. The only problem arises in the tails of the distributions, that show some residuals up to $\pm 0.6$ times the pixel pitch. The same thing can be observed in Fig. \ref{fig:mle_eef}, where it is possible to see that the EEF has very long tails, in particulat for small charge cloud sizes. Furthermore, it can be seen the same trend with the charge cloud size as the charge barycenter. This confirms that, as far as the position reconstruction is considered, a larger cloud dispersion is translated into a better accuracy, thanks to the larger amount of information available.

\subsection{$\eta$ algorithm}
\label{subsec:eta}
In the context of silicon microstrip detectors, a common and widely used algorithm to reconstruct the incident position of radiation is the so-called $\eta$ algorithm \cite{belau1983, turchetta1993}. This algorithm is based on the definition of the $\eta$ variable

\begin{equation}
\eta = \frac{q_R}{q_L + q_R},
\label{eq:def_eta}
\end{equation}
that for a microstrip detector is the ratio between the charge collected by the right strip and the total charge collected by the two strips involved in the event. By characterizing the distribution of this variable and studying the relation with the incident position, it is possible to obtain a non-linear algorithm, contrary to the charge baryecenter, that takes into account how the charge diffuses, like the maximum likelihood estimation with the charge diffusion model.

The typical distribution of $\eta$ is a U-shaped curve, peaked near $\eta = 0$ and $\eta = 1$ and symmetrical with respect to $\eta = 0.5$. Assuming that the events are uniformly distributed between the two strips, the cumulative distribution of the $\eta$ gives the relation between the impact point of the radiation and the observed value of $\eta$. The two distributions are shown in Fig. \ref{fig:belau_eta}.

As already said for the other reconstruction methods, for a one-pixel event there is nothing that can be done, thus the reconstruction position must coincide with the pixel center.

The case of two pixel events is more interesting, and it can be treated in a similar way as a microstrip detector. A small change in the definition of $\eta$ is necessary, which is now defined as

Defining these two new variables still is not enough, because they are not good to easily parametrize the problem. Looking at the disposition of the three pixel in Fig. \ref{fig:three_pixel_event}, a better parametrization can be employed to simplify the description of this type of events. The idea is to reduce a three-pixels event to a two-pixels event, where the central pixel remains the same, while the outer pixel becomes the sum of the two outer pixels. This automatically defines a new $\eta_+$ variable which is defined as

\begin{equation}
\eta_+ = \eta_1 + \eta_2,
\label{eq:eta_sum}
\end{equation}
which is related to the coordinate $r$, which is the distance of the incident position projected onto the axis that passes through the middle point between the outer pixels' centers.

Once the radial coordinate is defined, it is necessary to define also an angular coordinate $\theta$, which describes how far the incident position is from the latter axis. Even in this case it is possible to reduce the problem to a two-pixel event problem, where the interesting relation is between the two outer pixels. For this purpose, a new variable can be defined, and a possible definition is

\begin{equation}
\eta_- = \frac{\eta_1 - \eta_2}{\eta_+}.
\label{eq:eta_minus}
\end{equation}

Studying the $\eta_+$ and $\eta_-$ distributions and their cumulative distributions, it is possible to get a calibration for the radial and angular coordinates, which can then be used to reconstruct the photon incident position. An example of these distributions is shown in Fig. \ref{fig:eta_3pix}, and it can be noted that the shape of the distributions are very different from the two dimensional cases in Figs. \ref{fig:belau_eta} and \ref{fig:eta_2pix}.

The $\eta$ algorithm presented for the three-pixels case could also be generalized to all cluster sizes, because the only necessary thing to do is to identify the symmetry axes of the specific cluster size and then opportunely define two linear combinations of the $\eta_i$, like $\eta_+$ and $\eta_-$. Anyway, with an increasing number of pixels the charge barycenter reconstruction improves so, as already described in Sec. \ref{subsec:barycenter}, in the limit of a large number of pixels the gain obtained by the $\eta$ algorithm is negligible. For this detector, only the case up to three pixels has been covered, as it is designed to not have big cluster sizes to not deteriorate the energy resolution and three is the maximum accepted size for a cluster.

Similarly to the likelihood reconstruction method, the $\eta$ algorithm requires a calibration before being able to use it. The important difference between the two methods is that the $\eta$ algorithm does not need Monte Carlo truth information, but it can be directly calibrated from observed data. The only thing to be careful to is using a source that uniformly samples the hexagon, which is easily achievable with a large enough beam. This is really important when, for some reason, it is not possible to have a Monte Carlo simulation perfectly reproducing the detector, but the charge barycenter reconstruction accuracy is not enough.

The strenght of this method is that a Monte Carlo simulation is not necessary. However, in case it is avalaible, a different formulation of the $\eta$ algorithm can be made using the true incident positions. The charge diffusion model is necessary, but contrary to the likelihood method, some approximations can be considered and the diffusion process can be solved analytically. As said before, in the case of a two-pixel event the position can only be reconstructed along the line that connects the two centers. For this reason, the simplified uni-dimensional problem of a photon hitting the pixel exactly on this line can be considered. The charge diffusion can be modeled as a one-dimensional Gaussian distribution centered on the incident point at a distance $r$ from the central pixel. Given that the charge cloud dimension is small with respect to the pixel, all the charge is collected only by these two pixels. As a consequence, the fraction of charge $\eta$ collected by the outer pixel is the portion of the Gaussian that lies on the pixel

\begin{equation}
\eta(r) = \int ^{\infty}_{p/2} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma} \exp \left[ - \frac{(r' - r)^2}{2\sigma^2}  \right] dr' = \Phi \left( \frac{r - p/2}{\sigma} \right),
\label{eq:eta_2pix_int}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi(z)$ is the cumulative density function of the standard normal distribution and $p$ is the pixel pitch. Eq. \ref{eq:eta_2pix_int} can be inverted to find

\begin{equation}
r(\eta) = \frac{p}{2} + \sigma \Phi^{-1}(\eta),
\label{eq:eta_2pix_final}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi^{-1}(p)$ is the percent point function of the standard normal distribution. All the parameters in Eq. \ref{eq:eta_2pix_final} are known or can be calculated, but in the real case, where not all photons hit the central pixel exactly on the line connecting the centers, their value can change a bit. For this reason, a calibration is necessary and can be performed using Monte Carlo truth information. To perform it, the relevant quantity that has to be calculated is the projection of the true incident position onto the line that connects pixel centers. This quantity is calculated for each two-pixel event, then they are binned in $\eta$ and, for each value of $\eta$, the median of the $r$ distribution is computed. The choice to use the median has been made because these distributions have long tails, as shown in Fig. \ref{fig:eta_2pix_r_distr}.

A similar approach can be used for three-pixels events. As already said above, this case requires to separate between the radial and the angular coordinates, with both components that are approximable with   two-pixels problems. For the radial coordinate, where the outer pixels can be seen as a single pixel whose charge is the sum of their charges, the problem is almost identical to the one that has just been discussed. The only difference is the lower integration limit of Eq. \ref{eq:eta_2pix_int}, that this time corresponds to the vertex of the hexagon, which is located at $r=p/\sqrt{3}$. The calculation thus becomes
\begin{equation}
\eta_+(r) = \int ^{\infty}_{p/\sqrt{3}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma} \exp \left[ - \frac{(r' - r)^2}{2\sigma^2}  \right] dr' = \Phi \left( \frac{r - p/\sqrt{3}}{\sigma} \right),
\label{eq:eta_3pix_int}
\end{equation}
which leads to
\begin{equation}
r(\eta_+) = \frac{p}{\sqrt{3}} + \sigma \Phi^{-1}(\eta_+).
\label{eq:eta_3pix_no_final}
\end{equation}
There is a small correction to apply, because Eq. \ref{eq:eta_3pix_no_final} does not take into account the hexagonal geometry of the pixels and that the internal angles of the hexagons are 120$^{\circ}$. Indeed, if the charge would be equally divided between the three pixels, $\eta_+ = 2/3$ but $\Phi^{-1}(2/3) \neq 0$, thus the impact point would not be reconstructed on the vertex, but further from the central pixel. The solution is to normalize $\eta^+$ in order to correct this case, thus the model is
\begin{equation}
r(\eta_+) = \frac{p}{\sqrt{3}} + \sigma \Phi^{-1}\left( \frac{3}{4}\eta_+ \right).
\label{eq:eta_3pix_final}
\end{equation}

To find the model for the angular coordinate it is possible to only consider the two outer pixels, because $\eta_-$ is normalized with respect to the charge collected by these two pixels. The charge diffusion between these two pixels can still be modeled as a one-dimensional Gaussian distribution, centered in $y$, which is the distance from the midpoint of the pixels. Calculating the fraction of Gaussian that falls inside each pixel, it is possible to find the relation between $\eta_-$ and $y$, which is

\begin{equation}
\eta_-(y) = \frac{\eta_1}{\eta_+} - \frac{\eta_2}{\eta_+} = \left[1 - \Phi \left( - \frac{y}{\sigma} \right ) \right] - \Phi \left( - \frac{y}{\sigma} \right).
\label{eq:eta_3pix_ang}
\end{equation}
Using the approximation of small incident angles, the relation between the angular coordinate $\theta$ and $y$ is $\theta = y / r$. Inverting Eq. \ref{eq:eta_3pix_ang}, it is possible to find the relation between $\theta$ and $\eta_-$

\begin{equation}
\theta(\eta_-) =  - \frac{\sigma}{r} \Phi^{-1} \left( \frac{1 - \eta_-}{2} \right) = \frac{\sigma}{r} \Phi^{-1} \left( \frac{1 + \eta_-}{2} \right),
\label{eq:eta_3pix_ang_final}
\end{equation}
where it has been used that $\Phi^{-1}(1 - z) = - \Phi^{-1}(z)$.

To calibrate the radial and angular coordinates the strategy is identical to the calibration of two-pixels events. The projection onto the $\hat{r}$ axis and the angle with respect to it are calculated from Monte Carlo truth data of three-pixels events, then are binned in $\eta_+$ and $\eta_-$, and the median of the respective distributions are calculated. The resulting data are fitted with the respective models in Eqs. \ref{eq:eta_3pix_final} and \ref{eq:eta_3pix_ang_final}. Data and best-fit curves are shown in Fig. \ref{fig:eta_3pix_fit}.

\subsection{Modulation transfer function}
\label{subsec:mtf}

\fix{inserire citazioni \cite{samei1998}, \cite{med_imaging}.}

To characterize the imaging response of the detector, a method that is commonly applied in medical physics is to produce the image of a linear object. This can be achieved using an absorber material plate, such as lead for X-rays, with a slit on it \cite{qualcuno}. The intensity profile of the image on the axis perpendicular to the slit as a function of the position is then calculated, and the result is called line spread function (LSF). More precisely, the LSF is the convolution of the PSF with the slit

\begin{equation}
\operatorname{LSF}(x) = \operatorname{PSF}(x, y) \ast \operatorname{Slit}(x, y).
\end{equation}

It follows that in order to correctly characterize the resolution, the slit function should approach a Dirac $\delta$ function, thus the slit width must be smaller than the expected resolution. Another requirement for a better characterization is that the slit should be inclined with respect to the detector axes and the reason for this varies depending on the detector type. In case of energy integrating detectors, like the ones commonly used in radiography, the reason is that a slit aligned to an axis would produce a narrow LSF profile only few pixels wide, thus it would be under-sampled. Instead, using an inclined slit causes pixels in following rows to be hit in different positions, allowing to sample the LSF with sub-pixel sampling, which takes the name of presampled LSF. For single-photon driven readout detectors like ASIX, with which each photon is independently reconstructed with sub-pixel resolution, the reason of the inclined slit is not to increase the sampling, as the sub-pixel binning could be reduced to an arbitrary small dimension, but to uniformly sample the hexagonal pixel to estimate the average resolution of the detector on the entire pixel, and not only in a specific part of the pixel. The typical angle of the slit should be small, between 2 and 8 degrees, and should be far from all the possible main axes of the detector. An example of the geometry of the detector and the slits is shown if Fig. \ref{fig:hex_grid_slits}.

Producing a slit that matches the requirements of a high-resolution detector can not always be possible. The alternative method to measure the spatial resolution is to use the sharp edge of a slab of absorber material. The sharp edge is necessary to not introduce distorsions in the image. The process is similar to the calculation of the LSF, as the produced image is a linear region of transition between the free and the covered part of the detector. The profile of the transition takes the name of edge spread function (ESF). As for the slit method, the edge method requires the edge to be inclined with a small angle with respect to the main axes of the detector, for the reasons discussed above. Similarly to the LSF, the ESF can be computed as

\begin{equation}
\operatorname{ESF}(x) = \operatorname{PSF}(x, y) \ast \operatorname{Edge}(x, y).
\end{equation}

As mentioned above, the ideal edge should be perfetcly sharp, thus it can be represented with a step function. Using the convolution properties, it is possible to show that the derivative of the ESF corresponds to the LSF

\begin{equation}
\frac{d}{dx} \operatorname{ESF}(x) = \operatorname{PSF}(x, y) \ast \frac{d}{dx} \operatorname{Edge}(x, y) = \operatorname{PSF}(x, y) \ast \operatorname{Line}(x, y) = \operatorname{LSF}(y).
\label{eq:esf_lsf}
\end{equation}

Eq. \ref{eq:esf_lsf} is useful because once the LSF has been sampled, either with a slit or an edge, it is possible to characterize the profile to determine the spatial resolution, that can be defined as the full width at half maximum or alternatively, if the LSF can be described by a Gaussian curve, as its standard deviation. 

All the quantities that have been discussed above are defined in the spatial domain. There is another quantity that is commonly used to characterize the imaging properties of a detector, but defined in the frequency domain, the modulation transfer function (MTF). The MTF is defined as the Fourier transform of the LSF

\begin{equation}
\operatorname{MTF}(f) = \mathcal{F}(\operatorname{LSF}(x)).
\label{eq:mtf}
\end{equation}

By definition, the amplitude of MTF is normalized in order to have $\operatorname{MTF}(0) = 1$, which can be achieved either by normalizing the LSF area to 1 or by dividing the resulting MTF by $\operatorname{MTF}(0)$. The MTF measures how the contrast is transfered as a function of the spatial frequency of the incoming signal. A high value of the MTF for higher frequencies means that the detector has a high contrast, thus it can resolve well small objects. If the detector would ideally have infinite resolution, the LSF would be a Dirac $\delta$ function and the resulting MTF would have a constant value of 1. As for the LSF and the ESF, using an angled slit or edge to increase the sampling frequency allows to compute the presampled MTF.

The usual metric used to determine the spatial resolution of a detector is related to the frequency $f_{10}$ for which the $\operatorname{MTF}(f_{10})$ decreases to 10\% of modulation. If the LSF can be described by a Gaussian curve $f_{10}$ can be related to the standard deviation of the curve, using that the Fourier transform of a Gaussian is still a Gaussian

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F} \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi}\sigma} e^{-x^2/2\sigma^2 } \right) (f) = e^{-2 \pi^2 \sigma^2 f^2},
\label{eq:mtf_gauss}
\end{equation}
and imposing that $\operatorname{MTF}(f_{10}) = 0.1$ it can be found that
\begin{equation}
\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\ln 10}{2 \pi^2}} \frac{1}{f_{10}} \simeq \frac{1}{2.93 \cdot f_{10}}.
\label{eq:mtf_sigma}
\end{equation}

It is possible to have cases where the LSF is not Gaussian. A simple example is to consider an ideal detector, without noise of any kind, composed by a grid of square pixels. If the detector does not have any method to reconstruct sub-pixel positions, the LSF can be represented by a constant function in the region occupied by the pixel, say from $-d/2$ and $d/2$ for a pixel of size $d$, and zero elsewhere. When calculating the MTF from this LSF, the result is

\begin{equation}
\operatorname{MTF}(f) = \frac{\sin (\pi d f)}{\pi df} = \operatorname{sinc}(df).
\label{eq:mtf_square}
\end{equation}

When the MTF of a detector working as described above is described by Eq. \ref{eq:mtf_square}, it means that it has achieved the best spatial resolution possible. A similar discussion can also be made for hexagonal pixels to find the maximum theoretical resolution achievable by ASIX if no sub-pixel reconstruction algorithm would be employed, but all the signal would be assigned to the central pixel of the cluster. The two-dimensional $\operatorname{MTF}(f_x, f_y)$ of an hexagonal pixel has been calculated in \cite{barnard1991}, and for the directions orthogonal to the side and towards the vertex, considering a pixel with a pitch $p$ and with pointy-top orientation, they are equal to

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\operatorname{MTF}(f_x, 0) = \frac{2}{3} \left[ \operatorname{sinc}(p f_x) + \frac{1}{2} \operatorname{sinc}^2\left( \frac{p}{2} f_x \right) \right], \\
\operatorname{MTF}(0, f_y) = \operatorname{sinc} \left( 	\frac{p}{2 \sqrt{3}} f_y	\right) \operatorname{sinc} \left( 	\frac{\sqrt{3} p}{2} f_y	\right).
\end{split}
\end{equation}

In Fig. \ref{fig:mtf_pixels}, where the comparison between the MTF of a square pixel and that of an hexagonal pixel with the same area is shown, is possible to see that the latter geometry has the best resolution between the two. This can be related to the highest degree of isotropy and symmetry of the hexagon, which is more similar to a circle than a square is. Even if this calculation is made for an ideal detector with no sources of noise or blurring, for the ASIX hexagonal geometry it can be interpreted as the worst case scenario resolution, as there is no sub-pixel reconstruction, which widely improves the performance.

    \caption{MTFs calculated for square and hexagonal pixels with same area. The hexagonal pixel pitch is $p = 50$ $\mu$m. The MTFs referred to the hexagonal pixel are calculated along the directions towards the vertex and orthogonal to the side.}
    \label{fig:mtf_pixels}

A faster way to estimate the spatial resolution of a detector consists of using a line pair phantom, which is a plate with multiple sets of alternating slits with different widths and spatial frequencies, which are expressed as line pair or cycles per millimeter (lp/mm or cycles/mm). The phantom is made of an absorber material, usually lead, and when it's imaged the resolution can be estimated from the spatial frequency of the smallest set of slits that can be resolved. The only requirement for this method is to have a set of slits with a width smaller or comparable to the spatial resolution of the detector, but this may not be possible for high resolution detectors.

Going back to the MTF calculation, in case the slit or the edge is tilted, for the reasons discussed above, it is first necessary to calculate the angle to then correctly reproject the LSF or the ESF. For the moment it can be assumed to be working with a slit image, but analyzing an edge image is essentially the same. To find the angle, the slit is the only reference in the image and its angle with respect to the detector $x$-axis has to be computed. The first step of the process is to detect the slit in the image, meaning that its edges have to be detected. To accomplish this task, the Canny edge detection algorithm \cite{canny1986} has been employed. This algorithm allows to detect the edges in an image, and returns a binary image of the same size where a value of 1 means that a pixel belongs to an edge and 0 if it does not. Before running this algorithm it is necessary to create an image binning the reconstructed positions along the slit, and this process requires to choose the bin size. Another important parameter to adjust is the width of the Gaussian filter applied by the algorithm to smooth the image. The bin size and the filter width have to be carefully chosen in order to not be influenced by the noise, that would mean detecting edges that do not correspond to the slit. The ideal filter width, which is expressed in pixels, should be of the same order of the slit width, as can be observed in Fig. \ref{fig:mtf_canny}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/chapter4/position/mtf_canny}
    \caption{Example usage of the Canny edge detection algorithm on a simulated slit image. The simulated image (a) is binned with a $200 \times 200$ grid, while the slit has a Gaussian profile with a standard deviation of 5 pixels, a length of 160 pixels and a tilt angle of 5 degrees. Images from (b) to (f) show how the detected edges vary by changing the Gaussian filter width. A filter to small is very sensitive to low statistics and detects false edges. Increasing the filter width, this effect becomes less and less visible. If the filter is too wide with respect to the slit width, no edge is detected (case not shown).}
    \label{fig:mtf_canny}
\end{figure}

The second step of the process consists in calculating the angle from the detected edges, which are sets of straight lines. This can be performed with the use of the Hough transform \cite{hough1959, duda1972}, which is a technique used to detect parameterizable curves, such as lines. In this specific case, a line in the plane is represented by a set of two parameters $(\rho, \theta)$, which define the Hough space, where $\rho$ is the distance of the line from the origin of the axes and $\theta$ is the angle between the $x$-axis and the distance vector. Within this representation, each point $(x, y)$ on the line respects

\begin{equation}
\rho = x \cos \theta + y \sin \theta.
\label{eq:hough}
\end{equation}

To find the lines, for each point tested, the algorithm scans a given range of $\theta$ values, and for each one evaluates Eq. \ref{eq:hough}. The results are accumulated in a two-dimensional histogram, named accumulator space, where for each point and angle value an entry is added at the calculated $(\rho, \theta)$. A straight line is detected when a peak is identified in the latter space, and the tilt angle can be calculated as $\theta_{peak} - \pi/2$. An example of the result of the Hough transform of a slit edge obtained with the Canny edge detection algorithm is shown in Fig. \ref{fig:mtf_hough}. During the analysis, if multiple lines are present and multiple peaks are produced by the Hough transform, only the highest one is considered to calculate the tilt angle.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter4/position/mtf_hough}
    \caption{Accumulator space resulting from the Hough transform of the slit edge image from Fig. \ref{fig:mtf_canny}(e). The two nearly identical peaks that are visible correspond to the two long sides of the slits.}
    \label{fig:mtf_hough}
\end{figure}

To calculate the presampled LSF, the slit image has to be rotated with the angle found with the Hough transform. Once it has been aligned, the presampled LSF is calculated projecting all the points on the short side of the slit and creating the histogram of the reconstructed positions. The bin size of this histogram must be smaller than the expected resolution of the detector, in order to not incur in aliasing effects, as stated by the Nyquist theorem, but large enough to not be sensitive to statistical fluctuations of the measured positions. A possible way to limit effects from statistical fluctuations and noise is to apply some smoothing algorithm on the LSF, but this should be performed with caution as, in frequency domain, the smoothing acts as a low-pass filter, with the possible consequence of underestimating the actual detector resolution.

The presampled MTF is then obtained from Eq. \ref{eq:mtf} using a discrete fast Fourier transform algorithm.

In conclusion, the MTF represents the only available way to characterize the spatial resolution of ASIX, and this analysis can be performed both on real and simulated data, in order to verify the consistency between the difference of the various implemented reconstruction algorithm and also the consistency between data and simulations.
